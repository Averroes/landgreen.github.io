Magnetism

Magnetic Forces

A charged particle will experience a force when it moves in a magnetic field. The magnetic field is pointed out of the page.

$$ F=qvB sin \theta $$

F = magnetic force [N, Newton, kg m s⁻²] vector
q = charge [C, Coulomb] scalar
v = velocity [m s⁻¹] vector
B = magnetic field [T, tesla, kg C⁻¹ s⁻¹] vector
θ = angle between v and B scalar
orders of magnitude of teslas

Example: A +2C particle is moving at 10m/s through a 5mT field from a refrigerator magnet. What magnitude force will be applied to the particle?

solution $$ F=qvB $$ $$ F = (2)(10)(0.005) $$ $$ F = 0.1 N $$

Example: In order to learn about the strength of a magnetic field electrons are fired into the field at 100 m/s. The electrons accelerate at 1m/s². What is the magnitude of the magnetic field?

solution $$ F = ma $$ $$ F = (9.1 \times 10^{-31})(1)$$ $$ F = 9.1 \times 10^{-31}N$$
$$ F = q v B $$ $$ B = \frac{F}{qv} $$ $$ B = \frac{9.1 \times 10^{-31}}{(1.6 \times 10^{-19})(100)} $$ $$ B = 5.688 \times 10^{-14} T $$

Direction of Magnetic Forces

The 3-d nature of the magnetic force come from the vector operation of a cross product. We can find the direction with the right hand rule.

Using you right hand only place your thumb in the direction the charge is moving and place your index finger in the direction of the magnetic field. The palm of your hand will face in the correct direction of the force.

Example: A magnetic field is pointed North. A positively charged particle is moving down through the field. What direction is the magnetic force on the particle?

solution
Using the right hand rule place your thumb down and your index finger north. $$East$$

Example: What direction magnetic field would produce a downward force on a negatively charged particle that is moving west?

solution
A negatively charged particle will go in the opposite direction of a positively charged particle. Using the right hand rule place your thumb west east and the palm of your hand down. $$South$$

Example: A 1C charged particle is moving west at 10 m/s through a 2T magnetic field directed east. What is the magnitude and direction of the force on the particle?

solution

Since the velocity and magnetic field are in opposite directions there is no force.

$$F=q|v||B| sin (\theta) $$ $$sin(180) = 0$$ $$F=0$$

Example: Find the magnitude and direction of the force on a 24μC charge moving down at 13m/s through a 8.5T magnetic field directed South.

solution $$ F = q v B $$ $$ F = (24 \times 10^{-6})(13)(8.5) $$ $$ F = 0.00265 N \quad East$$

Sources of magnetic fields

A moving charge produces a magnetic field.

Einstein's theory of special relativity views electric and magnetic fields as parts of an electromagnetic field. Stationary charges only produce an electric field. Moving charges have a magnetic field in addition to the electric field.

Electric current in a straight wire produces a magnetic field that curves around the wire.

The right hand rule can be used to find the direction the field turns. Place your right hand's thumb in the direction of the current. Your finger will curl in the direct the field turns.




A spinning charge produces a magnetic field.

Electrons spin. Normally the electrons in atoms spin in opposing directions, but in ferromagnetic materials some spins remain unbalanced. The unbalanced spinning electrons produce a net magnetic field.

Permanent magnets always have unbalanced spins.
Example: an iron refrigerator magnet.

Paramagnetic magnets only have unbalanced spins when exposed to a magnetic field.
Example: the metal refrigerator door.

Earth's Magnetic Field

The earth produces a magnetic field that is closely aligned with it's axis of rotation. The source of the field is probably from internal convection of conductive materials in earth's core, but the process is complex and has only been recently modeled with computer simulations.

We also have strong evidence that the North and South poles experience periodic reversals. This means that a compass a million years ago with point in the opposite direction it points now. The reversals are unpredictable, but generally there is several thousand years of stability between flips.

The earth's magnetic field slows down the charged particles from space. A pretty example of this effect light given off by the solar wind interacting with the earth magnetic field can be best seen near the north and south poles and is called an aurora.

Electromagnetic Induction

Electromagnetic induction is the production of voltage from a conductor moving past a magnetic field. The process is used to make electricity in a generator at a power plant. In reverse it can also turn energy into motion in an electric motor.
