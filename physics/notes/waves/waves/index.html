Wave Terms

Waves have some properties that aren't always intuitive.

  • Waves don't have mass, but they do have energy.
  • Multiple waves can be in the same position at the same time.
  • Waves are always moving at a constant speed.
  • Waves expand in all directions.
    • Medium = the substance or material that carries the wave
    Propagate = transmit through a medium
    Amplitude = maximum displacement from rest (m)
    Crest = high point on a wave
    Trough = low point on a wave

    Wavelength = λ = the distance between adjacent crests or troughs (m)
    Period = T = the time for one complete wave cycle to pass a given point (s)
    Frequency = f = the number of cycles that pass per second (Hz, 1/s)

    Frequency and Period

    $$f = \frac{1}{T}$$

    \(f\) = frequency [Hz, s⁻¹, hertz]
    How often an event happens in a second.

    \(T\) = time period [s, seconds]
    How many seconds are between each event.

    If a wave has a frequency of 2Hz, then 2 wavelengths pass a point every second. The period would be 1/2 a second. This means it would takes 0.5 a second a single wave to pass.

    Example: The Los Angles Metro Expo Line has a train arrive at 6:50am, 6:56am, and 7:02. What is the period and frequency of the trains?
    solution $$6:56-6:50 = 6min$$ $$T = 6 min \left(\frac{60s}{1min}\right) = 360s$$ $$f = \frac{1}{360}$$ $$f=0.0027Hz$$
    Example: An air horn sounds at a frequency of 220 Hz. How many seconds pass between each wave crest?
    solution $$f = \frac{1}{T}$$ $$T = \frac{1}{f}$$ $$T = \frac{1}{220}$$ $$T = 0.0045s$$
    Example: Red light has a frequency of 450 THz. What is the period of red light. (THz = 10¹²Hz)
    solution $$f = \frac{1}{T}$$ $$T = \frac{1}{f}$$ $$T = \frac{1}{450 \times 10^{12}}$$ $$T = 2.22 \times 10^{-15}s$$

    Media and Wave Speed

    Waves propagate at a speed determined by their medium. Properties like frequency, amplitude, or wavelength generally don't determine the speed of propagation.

    Each type of wave has a different mechanism of propagation. The speed and possibility of a wave propagating through a medium is different for each wave type.

    speed (m/s) vacuum air water glass
    sound N/A 340 1,484 4,540
    light 300,000,000 300,000,000 220,000,000 200,000,000

    Sound travels faster for denser media, like water. The atoms are closer together. This means the atoms don't have to move as far to collide.

    Light always propagates at the fastest possible speed for anything. In dense transparent media, like water, it actually gets briefly absorbed by atoms and then re-emitted. This makes it seem like light slows down.

    Investigation: Can you figure out what factors affect the speed of a string wave? Go experiment with a string, or slinky to find out.
    solution Tension is probably the easiest way to control the speed of a string wave. The equation below shows that string mass and length are also factors. $$ v = \sqrt{\frac{T}{mL}}$$
    v = velocity (m/s)
    T = tension (N)
    m = mass (kg)
    L = string length (m)
    Changing wave speed is used to change the pitch of stringed instruments. Think about how notes from a guitar or harp are changed through tension, length, or string mass.
    Example: Two students are holding a slinky while standing 3.6 m apart. The first student sends a pulse which travels all the way down and back again. It takes 2.4 s for the wave to return. What is the speed of the wave?
    solution $$v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$$ $$v = \frac{3.6 \times 2}{2.4} $$ $$v = 3 \tfrac{m}{s}$$

    The Wave Equation

    For regularly repeating waves the product of the frequency and wavelength is equal to the wave's velocity.

    $$v = f \lambda \quad \quad v = \frac{\lambda}{T} $$

    \(v\) = wave speed [m/s]
    \(\lambda\) = wavelength [m, meters]
    \(f\) = frequency [Hz, s⁻¹, hertz]
    \(T\) = time period [s, seconds]
    Example: An air horn sounds at a frequency of 220 Hz. What is the wavelength of the sound wave?
    solution

    speed of sound at sea level = 340 m/s

    $$v = f \lambda$$ $$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$ $$\lambda = \frac{340}{220}$$ $$\lambda = 1.54m$$
    Example: A cell phone sends and receives microwave light at a wavelength of 1cm. How many cycles of a microwave signal pass through the phone in one second?
    solution

    cycles per second is frequency

    $$c = 3.0 \times 10^{8}$$ $$v = f \lambda$$ $$f = \frac{v}{\lambda}$$ $$f = \frac{(3.0 \times 10^{8})}{0.01}$$ $$f=3\times 10^{10}Hz$$
    Example: Gravity waves move at the speed of light. The first detected gravity wave was on 11 February 2016 when the LIGO and Virgo Scientific Collaboration observed gravitational waves originated from a pair of merging black holes. If you play the same frequency as sound you can hear a chirp..

    How big are gravity waves? Over the 0.2 second detection, the frequency increased from 35Hz to 250Hz. Calculate the starting and ending wavelength?

    solution $$ v = 3 \times 10^{8} m/s$$ $$v = f \lambda$$ $$\lambda = \frac{v}{f}$$ $$\lambda = \frac{3 \times 10^{8}}{35}$$ $$\lambda = \frac{3 \times 10^{8}}{35}$$ $$\lambda = 8,500,000m$$
    $$\lambda = \frac{3 \times 10^{8}}{250}$$ $$\lambda = 1,200,000m$$

    The wavelength ranged from 8,500km to 1,200km.

    We can also find the length of the entire signal.

    $$v = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$$ $$\Delta x = v\Delta t$$ $$\Delta x = (3 \times 10^{8}) (0.2)$$ $$\Delta x = 0.6 \times 10^{8} m$$

    The entire signal was 60,000 km long.
    That's about 5 Earths long.
