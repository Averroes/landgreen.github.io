Coulomb's Law

Electric Charge

Electrical Conduction

Current is the flow of charge. Some materials let electrons move through them easily and some don’t.

Conductors allow electrons to flow with low resistance.
Example: copper, steel, paper clips, tap water.

Insulators have high resistance to electrons.
Example: wood, rubber, plastics, distilled water.

Semi-conductors have a medium amount of resistance.
Example: Carbon, silicon, computer chips

Coulomb's Law

$$ F = \frac{k_{e}q_{1}q_{2}}{r^{2}} $$

F = electrostatic force [N, Newtons, kg m/s²] vector
ke = 8.987 × 10-9 = Coulomb's constant [N m²/C²] scalar
q = charge [C, Coulomb's] scalar
r = distance between the center of each charge [m, meters] scalar

The direction of the force is attractive if the charges are opposite and repulsive if the charges are the same.

The charges need to be stationary point sources.
